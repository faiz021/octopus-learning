Low resolution of perimetric testing

It’s impractical to test every location in the visual field so perimetry has low spatial resolution.

It’s also impractical to test using each possible light intensity so luminance resolution is also low.

Low resolution of perimetric testing

Ideally, the hill of vision would be drawn from an infinite number of test locations.

Like pixels in a digital camera sensor, the greater the number of test locations in a visual field test the higher the special resolution.

Practicalities mean that only sampling a limited number of locations is possible.

Light sensitivity

Similarly, the hill of vision would be drawn from a stimulus of continuously changing luminance.

However, time constraints in particular make this approach impractical, so again as with the number of stimuli locations, only some luminance levels are included in the test.

The effects of age and test location

It has already been established that normal sensitivity thresholds depend on the test location, with sensitivity higher in the centre than in the periphery.

Because normal sensitivity to light decreases with increasing age the hill of vision is also affected by age.

The effects of age and test location

Therefore, the normal hill of vision is higher in a 20 year old than in an 85 year old.

Such patient specific factors mean that sensitivity thresholds are difficult to interpret.

Normal reference values (normative database) are used to aid clinical visual field interpretation.

The effects of age and test location

Values for each age group and test location are included in a normative database.

The normative value is compared with the measured value for a given age group at each location.

The difference describes the loss in sensitivity and is referred to as the deviation or defect.

Objectivity, subjectivity and normal fluctuations

In terms of equipment and test conditions perimetry is regarded as an objective process.

The patient does however introduce a subjective element.

As a result there are always fluctuations among different normal individuals and between different measurements of the same individual over a short period of time.

Objectivity, subjectivity and normal fluctuations

The likelihood of seeing or not seeing a stimulus is probabilistic.

As the luminance increases, there is a gradual increase from “unseen” to “seen” responses.

The frequency-of-seeing (FOS) curve is used to measure fluctuations objectively.

The frequency-of-seeing curve

Normal fluctuation is not distributed in a uniform way across the visual field.

They tend to be smaller at the centre of the visual field and in areas of good vision when compared to areas of poor vision.

The probability of an individual perceiving a stimulus changes gradually from 0% to 100%.

So sensitivity thresholds are defined as the stimulus luminance that is perceived with a probability of 50%.

The frequency-of-seeing curve

The FOS curve provides the scientific definition of a light sensitivity threshold while taking fluctuation into account.

It shows the probability of perceiving a certain stimulus luminance.

The light sensitivity threshold is defined as the stimulus luminance that can be seen 50% of the time.

Fluctuation is quantified as the range of luminance at which the probability of seeing the stimulus is 0% to the luminance at which the probability of seeing the stimulus is 100%.

Summary

Perimetry has low spatial and luminance resolution because it’s impractical to test every location in the visual field with every possible light intensity.

Practicalities mean that only a limited number of sample locations and light intensities are possible.

Normal sensitivity to light decreases with increasing age.

A normative database is employed to aid clinical visual field interpretation.

The difference between the measured sensitivity and the normative value is the deviation or defect.

The frequency-of-seeing (FOS) curve is used to objectively measure fluctuations introduced by the subject.
